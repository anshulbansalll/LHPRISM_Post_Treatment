Introduction

Ecosystems around the world face many threats — things like habitat loss, climate change, and pollution [@gentili2021]. One problem that people often overlook is the spread of invasive species [@usda2024]. In New York State, this isn’t just a future worry. It’s something that scientists, state agencies, and local communities deal with every day [@nysdec2024].

This report uses field data from the Lower Hudson Partnership for Regional Invasive Species Management (LHPRISM) to look at where invasive species are found in the Lower Hudson Valley and how they are being managed [@nysdec2024]. To understand why this data matters, it helps to know the bigger picture: what invasive species are, how New York State organizes its response, and what official plans guide decisions about which species to focus on.

Background on Invasive Species

At its most basic, an invasive species is any organism — plant, animal, insect, fungus, or microbe — that establishes itself outside its native range and causes meaningful harm to the environment, economy, or human health [@mack2000; @usda2024]. The word “invasive” is doing a lot of work in that definition. Not every non-native species causes problems; in fact, most introduced species fail to survive at all in new environments [@mack2000]. They arrive without the adaptations needed to handle unfamiliar climates, predators, and food sources, and they quietly die out. The ones that do establish themselves and thrive are the exception — but when they do, the consequences can be severe and long-lasting [@gentili2021].

What makes certain species so successful in new environments often comes down to a lack of natural checks [@mack2000]. Back in their home range, they are kept in balance by predators, competitors, parasites, and other ecological forces that have co-evolved alongside them over thousands of years. When they land somewhere new, those controls disappear [@mack2000]. Native species in the invaded area have no evolutionary history with this newcomer and may not recognize it as a threat, compete with it effectively, or survive in its presence [@usda2024]. The invasive species, freed from its usual constraints, can reproduce rapidly, consume resources aggressively, and spread far beyond where it first appeared [@gentili2021].

The pathways through which invasive species move around the world are closely tied to human activity [@mack2000]. Global trade is one of the biggest drivers — cargo ships, shipping containers, and pallets of imported goods routinely carry hitchhiking organisms across continents and oceans [@usda2024]. The pet trade has introduced countless exotic animals into new regions, and when those animals escape or are deliberately released, some find conditions favorable enough to establish wild populations [@usda2024]. Ornamental horticulture has a long history of introducing plants that eventually escape garden boundaries and spread into natural areas [@mack2000]. Even recreational activities like hiking, boating, and fishing can accidentally transport seeds, larvae, or spores from one watershed to another [@nysdec2024].

The ecological damage caused by established invasive species takes many forms [@gentili2021]. Some are aggressive predators that decimate native prey populations before those populations have any chance to adapt [@mack2000]. Others are competitors that crowd out native plants or animals from food, space, and other resources they need to survive [@usda2024]. Over time, a successful invasion can fundamentally restructure an ecosystem — replacing native plant communities with monocultures, disrupting food webs, altering water cycles, and in the most severe cases, driving native species to local or even global extinction [@gentili2021; @mack2000]. Invasive species can also carry parasites and pathogens that spill over into native wildlife, compounding the damage further [@usda2024].

Beyond the ecological toll, invasive species impose enormous economic costs [@diagne2021]. Agricultural industries lose billions of dollars annually to crop damage from invasive pests and weeds [@diagne2021]. Forestry operations contend with invasive insects and diseases that kill commercially valuable trees [@mack2000]. Water infrastructure is disrupted by organisms like zebra mussels that clog intake pipes and alter aquatic chemistry [@usda2024]. And the cost of active management — detection, treatment, removal, and restoration — adds yet another layer to the financial burden [@diagne2021; @leung2002]. In densely populated and ecologically diverse states like New York, these costs are felt across sectors and communities [@nysdec2024].

New York is particularly exposed to invasive species pressure for several interconnected reasons [@nysdec2024]. It sits at the hub of one of the busiest transportation networks on the eastern seaboard, with major ports, airports, highways, and rail lines that move enormous volumes of goods and people [@nysdec2024]. Its landscape spans a remarkable range of ecosystems — coastal wetlands, deciduous forests, alpine zones, freshwater river systems — each of which represents a potential foothold for a different suite of invasive organisms [@nysdec2024]. And its long history of settlement, agriculture, and land use transformation has already altered many native habitats in ways that make them more vulnerable to invasion [@gentili2021]. Put all of that together, and it becomes clear why New York has invested significantly in building a statewide infrastructure for invasive species management [@nysdec2024].

Background on NYS DEC Invasive Species Programs and the PRISM System

The New York State Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC) works with many groups — such as resource managers, non-profit organizations, industry workers, recreation groups, and other state agencies — to deal with invasive species [@nysdec2024]. To help with this work, New York created eight Partnerships for Regional Invasive Species Management (PRISMs) [@nysdec2024]. These PRISMs cover the entire state and help reduce the damage invasive species cause to New York’s environment [@nysdec2016]. PRISMs were formed and funded under New York State law (Environmental Conservation Law 9-1705) [@nysdec2024].

PRISMs are groups of partners who work together to manage invasive species in different regions of New York [@nysdec2016]. They include people from federal and state agencies, environmental groups, scientists, industry, outdoor recreation, and local community members [@nysdec2024]. The DEC supports each PRISM through the Environmental Protection Fund, giving money to the organizations that host and coordinate them [@nysdec2024; @nysdec2016].

PRISMs carry out many important tasks, including:

Planning invasive species management for their region [@nysdec2016]

Running programs that help prevent new invasive species [@nysdec2016]

Surveying and mapping where invasive species are found [@nysdec2024]

Finding new invasions early and responding quickly [@nysdec2016]

Carrying out control and removal projects [@nysdec2016]

Restoring and monitoring habitats after treatment [@nysdec2024]

Teaching people about invasive species and their impacts [@nysdec2024]

Bringing partners together to coordinate work [@nysdec2016]

Recruiting and training volunteers [@nysdec2024]

Supporting research through citizen science with the Invasive Species Research Institute [@nysdec2024]

Reporting invasive species sightings to iMapInvasives [@nysdec2024]

Providing education and outreach to communities [@nysdec2016]

Since 2013, the DEC has contracted organizations to run all eight PRISMs across the state [@nysdec2016].

PRISMs deliver:

Regional planning for invasive species management [@nysdec2016]

Early detection and rapid response [@nysdec2016]

Eradication and control projects [@nysdec2024]

Education and outreach (with DEC-supported educators) [@nysdec2024]

Coordination among partners [@nysdec2016]

Volunteer training and involvement [@nysdec2024]

Citizen-science support for research [@nysdec2024]

The DEC is the main state agency responsible for environmental protection in New York, and invasive species management is part of its work [@nysdec2024]. Because invasive species spread across boundaries, New York created PRISMs so each region can coordinate its efforts [@nysdec2024].

The Lower Hudson PRISM (LHPRISM) covers the Lower Hudson Valley [@nysdec2024]. It uses trained staff and community volunteers who go into the field, record invasive species, help with treatment projects, and upload data to a shared statewide database [@nysdec2024]. This data is used for many environmental projects in the region [@finley2023].

Information and Background on the NYS Invasive Species Lists

Not every non-native species that shows up in New York needs immediate action [@leung2002]. Deciding which species deserve attention — and how urgent that attention should be — requires a clear system for evaluating risk [@leung2002]. New York State uses two main systems to do this: a regulatory list managed by the Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC) and a tiered priority system created through collaboration between the PRISM network, state agencies, and the New York Natural Heritage Program (NYNHP) [@nysdec2024; @nynhp2024].

The regulatory list, created under the Environmental Conservation Law, sorts species into two groups based on how harmful they are and what restrictions are needed [@nysdec2024].

Prohibited species are considered so damaging, and so unnecessary for any legal use, that they cannot be possessed, sold, bought, transported, or introduced in New York at all [@nysdec2024]. There are no permits for these species — they are simply banned [@nysdec2024].

Regulated species are more complicated [@nysdec2024]. They may have some allowed uses, but there are still rules in place to prevent them from spreading [@nysdec2024].

Before a species is added to either category, experts review scientific information about its ecological impacts, how quickly it spreads, how feasible it is to control, and any economic or social considerations [@leung2002; @nysdec2024].

Alongside this legal framework, NYNHP created a tiering system to help PRISMs and other managers decide where to focus their limited time and resources [@nynhp2024]. Because New York has hundreds of invasive species, the tiering system uses a structured method that combines iMapInvasives data, invasiveness assessments, and expert input [@finley2023]. A species only receives a tier if it is considered to have high negative impacts [@nynhp2024]. Species that are present but not very harmful do not get tier assignments [@nynhp2024]. The tier definitions were finalized in 2017 through a statewide collaboration, giving all PRISMs a shared set of priorities [@nynhp2024; @finley2023].

These tiers can change over time [@nynhp2024]. They depend on accurate data, so new reports or successful management efforts can shift a species’ tier [@finley2023]. This is why reporting new sightings to iMapInvasives is strongly encouraged — better data leads to better decisions [@nynhp2024]. For the species included in this report, their presence in the LHPRISM dataset means they have already been evaluated, found to be high-impact, and identified as priorities for management in the Lower Hudson region [@finley2023; @nysdec2024].

Understanding this background — the regulatory status, the tier level, and the management history of each species — is important for interpreting the data and understanding what it means for ongoing conservation work in this part of New York State [@nysdec2024; @nynhp2024].

        • biennial lifecycle, plants 2+ yrs old 10-15ft tall

        • treatment recommendation: chemical treatment in early spring, mechanical removal for small infestations
      • PUMO - Kudzu
        • native to East Asia, widespread in SE USA and migrating N
        • thrives on roadside, forest edges, warm and humid climates, prefers full sun, climbs to canopy
        • perennial vine, grows up to 1ft/day in optimal conditions
        • spreads vegetatively, rooting at nodes
        • forms dense monocultures, causes tree death by shading and structural stress
        • alters nutrient cycle by nitrogen fixation
        • treatment complex, requires multi-modal approach over multiple years

        • Native to Europe, widespread invasiveness in PNW, California, and East Coast USA
